Porco's Spaghetti

Description

A humble, hearty spaghetti—just like the one Gina might have served Porco on a quiet afternoon at the Adriano. This dish captures the essence of Porco Rosso: rustic, sun-drenched, and comforting, with just a touch of melancholy. A meal best made slowly, with music playing and nothing urgent to do, this tomato-based pasta feels like an edible poem to lost time, long conversations, and slow flights home. The kind of food that carries you—gently—into your next scene.

Ingredients

  • Oil to coat the bottom of the pot and a little for the pasta
  • 1 can of crushed tomatoes
  • ¼ large white onion, diced
  • 2–3 garlic cloves
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 2–3 bay leaves
  • ½ tablespoon Italian spices
  • ½ teaspoon (or less) sugar
  • ½ lb of spaghetti
  • Fresh sweet basil (optional, to taste)

Steps

  1. Heat oil in a pot over medium-low heat. Add the diced onions and cook for 3–5 minutes, until they are soft and slightly translucent (but not browned).
  2. Add the garlic and continue cooking on low heat for another 3 minutes. Everything should be soft and lightly golden—avoid browning, as it will turn bitter.
  3. Pour in the crushed tomatoes and cook over medium-low heat for about 10 minutes, stirring occasionally.
  4. Add the bay leaves, Italian spices, salt, and pepper. If you have fresh basil, add it here. Stir everything together and let it simmer for another 10–15 minutes. Start with a small amount of seasoning and adjust gradually—it's easier to add than to remove.
  5. Taste the sauce and adjust as needed. Add a small pinch of sugar to balance the acidity. Simmer for another 5 minutes, then turn off the heat and let the sauce rest.
  6. While the sauce rests, bring a pot of water to a boil for the pasta. Once boiling, add a pinch of salt and a dash of oil to prevent sticking and add flavor.
  7. Cook the spaghetti until al dente, about 9–10 minutes. If you prefer softer pasta, leave it in slightly longer.
  8. Strain the pasta. If the sauce has cooled, reheat it gently before serving. Toss the pasta with the sauce, and it's ready to serve.
  9. This dish is even better if the sauce is simmered longer or rested for a few hours. On Sundays, we often let it sit for 2–3 hours—especially when paired with meatballs or braciole.
